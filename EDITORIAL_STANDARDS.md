# The London Jazz List — Editorial Standards

## The Core Limitation

The automated enricher can identify facts, credentials and lineage from the web.
It cannot hear music. It cannot judge whether a pianist has feel, whether a groove
is sloppy, or whether a room comes alive. All quality judgements — editors picks,
description tone, hidden flags — are the editor's, not the algorithm's.

**These standards exist to make the enricher's output match the editor's voice as
closely as possible, and to flag clearly where human judgement is required.**

---

## The Benchmark Description

**Camille Bertault** — this is the gold standard every description is measured against:

> "French vocalist who became a YouTube sensation with her scat improvisation over
> Coltrane's Giant Steps. Live she's electrifying — her voice an instrument of
> extraordinary range and wit."

Why it works: one specific verifiable fact (Giant Steps video), one evaluative claim
earned by the fact, no wasted words, doesn't start with the artist name, doesn't
say "jazz".

---

## Description Length as Enthusiasm Signal

Length and warmth should calibrate the reader's expectations, not just convey facts.

| Context | Length | Tone | Example |
|---|---|---|---|
| Editors pick | 2–3 sentences | Warm, specific, makes the case | Camille Bertault |
| Solid recommended gig | 2 sentences | Factual, one credential | Leo Richardson Quartet |
| Good but not exceptional | 1 sentence | Neutral, informative | "Royal Academy graduates." |
| Weak end of pro | 1 sentence | Honest, non-committal | "Tasteful sax-led band." |
| Youth / student band | 1 sentence | Plain, factual | "Youth big band." |
| Series / programme gig | 1 sentence | Standard formula | "Series promoting recent jazz school graduates." |
| Free improv | 1 sentence | Dry, insider-coded | "Hardcore European free improv." |
| No description warranted | blank | — | MOPOMOSO (title is self-explanatory) |

**The absence of enthusiasm is itself information.** A reader who knows the scene
will understand what "Royal Academy graduates" means without further comment.

### Editorial voice notes

- "Royal Academy graduates" — for technically trained players whose live quality
  doesn't warrant more. Neutral, not disparaging.
- "First rate Royal Academy of Music graduate" — for players where the credential
  genuinely reflects the live quality (Misha Mullov-Abbado).
- "Czech pianist joins UK pros" — model for international guest + solid local band
  without name-dropping.
- "Hardcore European free improv. Three sets... anchored by Smyth's long-standing
  trio with bassist John Edwards and drummer Mark Sanders." — model for free improv:
  name the musicians for those who know the world, say nothing to those who don't.

## Banned Phrases — Automatic Rejection

Any of these in a generated description means rewrite or skip:

- `"rarely does a..."` — generic, no facts
- `"make you forget your phone"` — no facts
- `"one of London's most compelling..."` — applies to anyone
- `"before the rest of the world catches on"` — applies to anyone
- `"rewards both first-timers and seasoned ears"` — applies to anything
- `"name-dropping in five years"` — applies to anyone
- `"rare authority"` — reserved for confirmed masters only (e.g. Kamasi Washington,
  Iain Ballamy). Do not use for anyone else, regardless of credentials.
- `"rare talent"`, `"rare gift"`, `"rare ability"` — same rule as above
- `"captivating presence"`, `"compelling performer"`, `"extraordinary talent"` —
  banned entirely, generic
- `"rare chance to catch/hear"` — only legitimate for artists who genuinely play
  London once a year or less (international star on a one-off visit). A London-based
  musician who gigs monthly is never a "rare chance". Just wait til next month.
- `"virtuoso"` — reserved for musicians who genuinely redefine what is possible
  on their instrument (Art Tatum, Jacob Collier). Everyone has to be technically
  accomplished to be in the game now. A wedding and corporate events musician
  listing themselves as a virtuoso on their own website is not a virtuoso.
- `"boundary-pushing"` — overused, meaningless
- `"cinematic textures"` — enricher cliché
- Never start with the artist name
- Never say `"jazz"`

## What Makes a Verifiable Fact

A description must contain at least one of:

- Named album on a real label (e.g. *"debut album 'New Life' on Bridge The Gap"*)
- Specific famous collaborator (e.g. *"played with Shabaka Hutchings"*)
- Country of origin or specific training (e.g. *"Guildhall-trained"*, *"São Paulo-born"*)
- Named award or publication credit with specific detail
  (e.g. *"Mercury Prize nominated"*, *"four stars from Jazzwise"*)
- Specific genre lineage with named figures
  (e.g. *"rooted in the Ornette Coleman tradition"*)
- Specific album/tour/broadcast credit
  (e.g. *"recorded with The Cinematic Orchestra"*, *"BBC Radio 3 airplay"*)

**Not verifiable facts:**
- "one of the UK's finest..." — opinion without anchor
- "critically acclaimed" — without naming the critic or publication
- "internationally renowned" — without naming the countries or context
- Press quotes recycled uncritically — a four-star Guardian review of a studio
  album does not mean the live show is worth attending

---

## Credentials ≠ Live Quality

This is the central editorial judgement the enricher cannot make.

- **Conservatoire training** signals technical foundation, not feel or spark.
  "Royal Academy graduates" is accurate and non-committal — let the reader decide.
- **Press quotes** from jazz critics reviewing albums grade on a different curve
  from what you'd experience in a room at 9pm on a Wednesday.
- **Being in a famous band** is not the same as being a star of it.
  (Julian Nicholas was in Loose Tubes; Iain Ballamy and Django Bates were its stars.)
- **Four-star reviews** + **small room booking** is a data point. Trust the booking
  over the review.
- **Wynton Marsalis / famous name associations** in a bio — check how central the
  connection actually was. Playing on a session or sharing a festival bill is not
  the same as a genuine musical relationship.

## Tier 1 UK Jazz Artists

Very few musicians qualify. Tier 1 means genuinely world-class, not just well-regarded
in London. Current examples:

- **Shabaka Hutchings** — Sons of Kemet, The Comet Is Coming, international profile
- **Kamasi Washington** — rare authority (not UK-based but plays here)
- **Iain Ballamy** — rare authority, decades of distinctive work
- **Django Bates** — same

Most working London jazz musicians, however credentialled, do not qualify. The
editors_pick flag is about whether this specific gig is worth recommending — not
whether the artist has a good CV.

## Venue & Room Tiers

**Tier 1 — Premier:**
Vortex, Ronnie Scott's (main stage), 606 Club, Barbican Hall, Cadogan Hall (main hall),
Royal Albert Hall (main hall), Lauderdale House, Jazzlive, World Heart Beat,
Bull's Head Barnes, Toulouse Lautrec, East Side Jazz, Highams Park Jazz,
Queen Elizabeth Hall, Royal Festival Hall, Wigmore Hall, King's Place

**Tier 2 — Good but secondary:**
Cadogan Hall Culford Room, Royal Albert Hall Elgar Room, PizzaExpress Jazz Club,
smaller independent venues with professional programming

**Tier 3 — Small/obscure:**
Karamel — small, low-profile, mixed quality. Gigs here should have minimal descriptions.
One sentence only.

**Sub-rooms matter:** Cadogan Culford Room ≠ Cadogan main hall. Elgar Room at the
RAH ≠ the main RAH stage. The Out to Lunch lunchtime series at Cadogan includes
student-level players — being at Cadogan does not automatically mean editors_pick.

## Auto Editors Pick — Exclusions

The following should **never** be auto-eligible for editors_pick regardless of
venue tier or price:

- Experimental / Free genre — *"I would very rarely recommend free improv to anyone.
  That would be evil."*
- Youth bands / student ensembles
- Whirlpool series gigs (unless manually overridden)
- Any gig at a tier 3 venue

---

## The Fundamental Rule

> A blank description is better than a generic one.
> A short honest description is better than a long flattering one.
> The description should reflect what you'd experience on the night —
> not what the PR says, not what the album review said, not what the bio claims.

## Listener Accessibility Notes

These are editorial judgements the enricher cannot make — they require actually
hearing the music or understanding how audiences experience it.

**Harmony instruments matter for accessibility.** Bands without a harmony instrument
(piano, guitar, vibraphone) — i.e. sax/trumpet + bass + drums only — are harder
for general audiences to follow. Expert jazz musicians can hear implied harmony
in their imagination. Most listeners cannot. This is not a quality judgement; it's
an accessibility one. Factor it into how warmly you describe the gig.

**Experimental and free improv gigs** serve a self-selecting audience of roughly
10 people who already know they want to go. The description should be dry and
informational, not welcoming. They don't need encouragement. Everyone else
needs a gentle signal that this is not for them.

**Comeback acts** with a long gap are genuinely unknown quantities — the 16-year-ago
version of the band is not the current version. Describe them as such rather than
recycling old credentials.

**"Same 10 people already waiting outside"** — the free improv audience. They are
loyal, knowledgeable and self-sufficient. Write for them, not for general readers.

## Naming Musicians in Descriptions

**Only name sidemen if the reader will recognise them.**

A name in a description is a signal to the reader: *this person means something*.
Listing unknown names is noise — it looks like credential-padding and adds no
information.

**Name a musician if:**
- They are a well-known bandleader in their own right
  (e.g. "with John Edwards on bass" — Edwards is a known figure in free improv)
- They have a specific credential the reader will recognise
  (e.g. "drummer Jay Davis, also of Mark Lockheart's Shapeshifter")
- Their presence is the actual reason to go
  (e.g. a famous guest joining a lesser-known leader)

**Don't name musicians if:**
- They are working sidemen without independent recognition
- The names are lifted from a press release to pad the description
- The reader would have no way to evaluate the name

**Don't repeat what's already in the title.** If the artist name is in the title,
don't restate it in the description. If the venue is shown separately, don't
mention it in the blurb.

**Lunchtime gigs** — a distinct format worth noting. Different audience, different
proposition from an evening show. Tag with `format_tags: Lunchtime`. If enough
accumulate, may warrant a dedicated section alongside brunches.
